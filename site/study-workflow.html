Study Workflow

Turn a lesson into a useful home coding session

Use the study workspace during a lesson. Its job is to leave you with a small, evidence-oriented next step, not to replace the implementation work you will do later.

1. Think from memory

Read a small section, then pause. Explain the primitive in one sentence, identify its invariant, name one failure it prevents, and choose the evidence that would establish confidence.

Do not copy lesson prose into your notes. Fragments and rough explanations reveal what you actually understand.

2. Plan one narrow slice

Use the Plan tab before leaving the lesson. Record the function or artifact, the smallest behavior, what it must do, what it must not do, the first proof, and one fuzzy question.

Target: run_agent
Smallest slice: stop on submit
Must not: execute an external tool
First proof: test_submit_stops_loop

3. Close the loop

Before leaving, write the one mental-model correction you want to retain and the next smallest action for when you are home. Mark the lesson ready to implement only when the plan is concrete enough to begin without rereading everything.

Low-energy version

When you have little time, capture only five things: the concept, five fragments, a plain-English explanation, the first proof, and one fuzzy question. This preserves momentum without turning study into administration.

What counts as progress

  • You can explain the primitive without looking.
  • You know the smallest behavior to build later.
  • You know what evidence should prove that behavior.
  • You recorded uncertainty instead of hiding it.
